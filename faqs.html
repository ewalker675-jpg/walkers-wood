How much firewood do I need?

It depends on how often you burn and the size of your stove or fireplace. As a rough guide:

Occasional use (weekends and evenings): 1–2 cubic metres should last a full winter season.
Regular use (daily heating): 3–4 cubic metres is a good starting point.
Primary heat source: You may need 5+ cubic metres per season.

If you're not sure, just get in touch and we'll help you work out what you need.

Lighting & using your fire

What's the best way to light a fire?

We recommend the top-down method — it's cleaner, produces less smoke, and gets your fire going more efficiently:

1. Place 2–3 larger logs on the base of your stove or fireplace.
2. Add a layer of smaller kindling on top in a criss-cross pattern.
3. Place a natural firelighter on top and light it.
4. Leave the door slightly ajar (if using a stove) for the first 5 minutes to build airflow.

The fire burns downwards, producing very little smoke and lighting quickly. Kindling is available as an add-on when you select a firewood product — just add it when prompted during checkout.

Which wood burns best?

Ash is widely considered the best all-round firewood — it burns hot, clean and steadily, and produces very little smoke. Oak burns longer and is great for sustained heat overnight. Cherry lights easily and gives a lovely bright flame, but burns faster.

Our hardwood mix typically includes a blend of these species, giving you the best of all worlds — easy lighting, strong heat and a long, steady burn.

How should I store my firewood?

Keep your firewood in a well-ventilated, covered area — a log store, open-fronted shed or lean-to is ideal. The key is to keep rain off the top while allowing airflow around the sides to prevent moisture from building up.

Avoid storing logs directly on the ground — use a pallet, rack or gravel base to keep them raised. And try not to stack them right up against a wall, as air needs to circulate on all sides.

How do I look after my wood burner?

A few simple habits will keep your stove performing well for years:

Empty the ash pan regularly — a small layer of ash actually helps insulate the firebox, but too much restricts airflow.
Have your chimney swept at least once a year, ideally before the burning season starts.
Burn only dry, seasoned or split and dried wood — wet or unseasoned wood causes tar buildup and can damage your flue.
Check the door seals and glass periodically and replace gaskets when they become worn.

Burning Woodsure Ready to Burn firewood (like ours!) significantly reduces the amount of soot and tar produced, keeping your stove and chimney in better condition.
